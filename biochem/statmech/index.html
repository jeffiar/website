Statistical Mechanics

A cell is made of lots of atoms moving around, so before learning about biology, we ought to learn about the physics of many-body systems.

The language of statistical mechanics describes the collective behavior of many interacting atoms. It helps us answer questions such as:
  • Of all the copies of protein ABC in a cell, what fraction are in an active conformation vs an inactive conformation?
  • What does it mean for a protein to "bind tightly" to something? For instance, antibodies from our immune system need to recognize and stick to "bad guys", transcription factors need to bind to genes to turn them on and off, etc.
  • How long does it take for a metabolite to diffuse from one end of a cell to another?
  • Why (and how!?) do our cells maintain differing concentrations of ions and metabolites on different sides of a membrane?
  • How do our cells drive chemical reactions that cost energy to perform?
As we'll see, the main idea behind stat mech is that when many atoms interact together, most of the atomic details end up becoming irrelevant. The collective behavior is instead summarized by a few "thermodynamic variables" that control biological behavior, such as the temperature and the chemical potential. Since there's so many atoms jiggling around and trading energy with each other, we can describe their behavior statistically; even though the motion of individual atoms appears random, the overall macroscopic behavior is essentially deterministic, and is described by these thermodynamic variables.

Statistical mechanics is a powerful framework to understand the driving forces behind cellular behavior. It tells us why certain processes are favorable and others are not. In the subsections below, we'll learn how to think about protein-protein binding, the energetics (and "entropics") of concentration gradients, diffusion, and more.
