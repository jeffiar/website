Prelude No. 1 for piano, Op. 4

May 2016
After reading Dostoevsky's Crime and Punishment in my high school AP lit class, I was inspired to write a prelude for piano (6') about the psychology of guilt, suffering and redemption.



The piece traces Raskolnikov's psychological development throughout Crime and Punishment, portraying agony, suffering, and ultimately redemption. It opens in a tormented state of mind, characterized by a nervous, uneasy pick-up rhythm. After some long, chromatic lines, the piece transitions into a second section of quiet suffering and sorrow, based on a motif of a slowly descending scale in uneven rhythm. During a long chorale, hints of the guilt theme resurface in the bass, as if Raskolnikov cannot escape what he has done. Eventually, the chorale transforms into a major key and melts into the third and final theme of hope and redemption. As the singing melody slowly fades away into silence, the last few notes ring the theme of inescapable guilt once more. After a long journey of agony and suffering, Raskolnikov may have redeemed his soul, but he will never escape his sin.

Horn Sonata No. 2, Op. 3

Feb 2016
To commemorate my high school horn teacher and his rather idiosyncratic technical exercises, I composed a horn sonata where the melodic motifs were derived from the exercises! It's a pretty whimsical piece, written in sonata allegro form, with a harmonically adventurous development and musical jokes littered throughout.
Unfortunately, I don't have a good recording of the piece, but you can take a look at the score here.
